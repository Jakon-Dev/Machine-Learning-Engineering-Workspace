# Anomaly Detection

Anomaly detection (also known as outlier detection) is the identification of rare items, events, or observations which raise suspicions by differing significantly from the majority of the data.

## Core Concept
In most cases, the "normal" data is abundant, while the "anomalous" data is extremely scarce. This makes it different from standard classification because the target class is often unlabeled or too imbalanced.

## Mathematical Intuition
**Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM):**
Assumes that normal data is generated by a mixture of several Gaussian distributions. Anomaly score is based on the probability density $p(x)$:
$$p(x) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \phi_k \mathcal{N}(x | \mu_k, \Sigma_k)$$
If $p(x) < \epsilon$, the point is an anomaly.

**Isolation Forest:**
Isolates observations by randomly selecting a feature and then randomly selecting a split value. Anomalies are easier to isolate and thus have shorter path lengths in the tree.

## Use Cases
- Credit card fraud detection.
- Intrusion detection (network security).
- Manufacturing quality control (defective parts).

## Advantages & Disadvantages
- **Advantages**: Works well even without labeled anomalies, effective for "unknown unknowns."
- **Disadvantages**: Hard to define the threshold $\epsilon$, sensitive to noise in the "normal" training data.

# Quick Challenge
**Objective:** Detect fraudulent credit card transactions.
**Dataset:** `Credit Card Fraud Detection` dataset (Kaggle).
**Evaluation Metric:** Since classes are highly imbalanced, do NOT use accuracy. Aim for an **Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC)** of at least **0.8**.
